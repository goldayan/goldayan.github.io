Measure of central tendency

  • Mean or Average sum of data / number of data items
  • Median The middle number if we lined up our data from smallest to largest.
  • Mode The value that appears most in our data set.

Distribution

A distribution shows us how often each value occurs in our data set, which also known
Distribution tell us about the shape and spread of data.

  • Normal

    • A Distribution of data that has roughly the same amount of data on either side of the middle, and has its most common values around the middle of the data.

    • Symmentric bell shaped graph

    • mean and median and mode are same

    • mean tells the centre of the distribution

    • standard deviation tells us whether the distribution is thin or broad.

    • unimodal ( have one peak )

    • distribution where data are normally distributed.

    • Central limit theorm

      • The distribution of sample means for an independent random variable will get closer and closer to a normal distribution as the size of the sample get bigger and bigger. Even if the original population distribution isn't normal itself.

  • BiModal or multimodal distribution

    • have two or more peaks

  • uniform distribution

    • same frequency

  • Binomial distribution

    • Binomial coefficeient formula makes it easy for us to find out how many ways a certain ratio of success eg: Not getting shocked by toaster - To failures eg: getting shocked by toaster can occur.
    • nCk
    • Bernouil's principle

  • Geometric distribution

    • geom (k;p) = (1-p)^k-1 * p
    • it tells that your first success will be on your nth try.

  • T distribution

    • A continuous probability distribution that's unimodal.
    • It's useful way to represent sampling distribution.

Extra things about Distribution and Central tendency

  • normal distribution has Zero skew since mean and median are same
  • when mean and median are different, a distribution is skewed ( unusaually extreme values on one side of our distribution ) here mode will be highest point in distribution, median will stay in the middle, but the mean will pulled towards the unusual
  • Mean lot higher than Median -> value (or values) that are relatively large in our data set.
  • Mean lot lower than Median -> value (or values) that are relatively small in our data set.

Measure of Spread

  • Range Range takes the largest number in dataset and subtract with smallest number in the set to give us the distance between these two extremes.The larger the distance, the more spread out our data is.

  • InterQuartile Range(IQR) The IQR looks at spread of the middle 50% of your data.

  • Variance

  • Standard deviation The average amount we expect a point to differ( or deviate) from the mean.

  • Variability

Randomness

  • since individual values of these random variables aren't that predictable so we generally look at multiple outcome
  • behaviour of random value through Simulation
  • Our best guess to a variable is mean
  • we can find the how spread of data expect to be is E(x-Mean)^2 ( second moment )
  • when we raise the power of E(x-mean) we can see different behaviour of data
  • E(x-mean)^3 is called skewness it tell us extereme value in one side or other side.
  • E(x-mean)^4 kurtosis - how thick the tail in the distribution are.
  • Normal distribution has kurtosis greater than 3 it is called positive below than 3 called negative.

Compare two different things

  • Z score allow us to compare things that are not on the same scale, as long as they are normally distributed.
  • Z score tell us the distance between the the mean of the distribution and a data point in standard deviations.
  • Percentiles
    • tell you the percentage of the population has a score or value that's lower than yours.

Confidence Intervals

  • An Estimated range of values that seem reasonable based on what we've observed. It's center is still the sample mean,but we have got some room on either side for our uncertainty.
  • Quantify our uncertainity.

Null Hypothesis Significance testing

  • A form of the reductio ad absurdum argument which tries to discredit an idea by assuming the idea is true, and then showing that if you make that assumption something contradictory happens
  • Simply take a argument or idea that is true and try to make it false.

P-value

  • alpha which is 0.05
  • if p-value is less than alpha then we reject null hypothesis.
  • else if p-value not lower than our pre-selected cut off ( alpha value ) then we fail to reject the null hypothesis.
  • Based on above statement we can reject or fail to reject sometimes we predict wrongly
  • wrong prediction leads to error
    • Type I => Rejecting the null, even if it is true. it can therefore only happen if the null is true.Essentially false positive.
    • Type II => failing to reject null hypothesis when it actually is false. false negatives.
  • we can find which distribution might be correct. we compare null distribution and alternative distribution.
  • Statistical power tells us our chance of detecting an effect if there is one.
    • affected by null and alternative overlap.more they overlap less the power will be.
